Two examples show the kind of risk that comes with running on decade-old, unmaintained technology. The platform has run without a major incident so far, but with no maintenance or updates in years, that track record reflects luck more than resilience — it can keep working right up until the moment it doesn't, and neither example below is a fire burning today. Both are the sort of thing that lands without warning on a platform no one is keeping current:

Example — latent fault

A billing bug documented in the platform — a card/billing-address mismatch that can block clients from submitting — has sat unaddressed for years. It isn't currently causing problems, but on a system this old, with no one maintaining it, that's fortune rather than safety. It's exactly what hardening is meant to catch before it resurfaces.

Example — vendor deadlines don't wait

Microsoft is retiring an older Azure storage type (GPv1) on October 13, 2026. We haven't yet confirmed whether our production storage is affected — but this is the kind of vendor-forced deadline that periodically lands on aging infrastructure, on someone else's schedule, ready or not.

Decision

Three committed months, then it depends how capital arrives

Only the first ~3 months are committed: Phase 1 hardening and a parallel fundraising push, both wrapping up around November. That next raise is targeted at $3–5M total — it could land all at once, or in stages, with an opening ~$1M tranche funding Phase 2 before the rest arrives for Phase 3, rather than Phase 2 needing separate capital on top of that $3–5M. If a more modest ~$1M lands first — as its own raise or as that opening tranche — Phase 2's prototype and beta validation run before the full build. If the full $3–5M raise arrives directly, Phase 3's full build starts immediately and Phase 2's beta validation runs alongside it instead of gating it. If neither has landed, we keep operating on the hardened platform — no new build — and keep raising, rather than spin up a team we can't sustain. Nothing past November is a commitment; it's a branch that resolves on how and when the money arrives.

Risk — the $1M tranche lands, the rest doesn't

"Phase 2, then Phase 3" assumes the remaining $2–4M follows before the $1M tranche's buffer runs out. If it stalls instead, this isn't the same as "keep operating as-is" — we'd be supporting a validated prototype for 5–10 beta clients while the other ~25–30 clients stay on the merely-hardened legacy platform, with no funded path to the full build. What we'd actually do in that scenario — pause onboarding, attempt a smaller follow-on raise, or fold Phase 2's learnings back into the existing platform instead of a separate full build — isn't decided yet, and needs an answer before we commit to this path.

Recommendation

Fund Phase 1 now. Which phase comes next depends on how capital arrives.

Why this sequencing

Phase 1 is the only one of the three sized to what this raise leaves for technology. It's aimed at the biggest operational risk — the current platform failing under its own weight — without betting on money we don't have yet. Moving on to Phase 2 or 3 would depend on additional fundraising, not this raise.

That second raise — targeted at $3–5M total — would be pursued in parallel, starting now rather than after Phase 1 wraps up. How it lands, not just whether it does, determines what happens next: it could arrive all at once, or in stages. If it arrives in stages, an initial ~$1M tranche of that same target funds Phase 2's prototype and beta validation first, with the full build following once the rest of that capital comes in — Phase 2 isn't necessarily capital on top of the $3–5M, it can be the first slice of it. If the full $3–5M arrives directly instead, the full build starts immediately, with Phase 2's beta validation running alongside it rather than as a prerequisite.

Neither Phase 2 nor Phase 3 is just a technology budget. Scaling past Phase 1 — onboarding 50–100+ clients and standing up Legacy Funds — also means scaling the team: this can't stay a one-person product/technology/operations operation, and there are real, currently unpriced costs in hiring, compute/storage, cybersecurity, and compliance that any Phase 2 or 3 raise needs to account for.

Timing is a factor: a Legacy Fund could need the platform as early as Q1–Q2 2027 — potentially only one to four months after Phase 1 would finish. That's a thin margin, which is the argument for starting the next raise sooner rather than waiting for more clarity. All of these dates and costs are estimates.
